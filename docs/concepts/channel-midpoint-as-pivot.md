# Concept: Channel Midpoint as Pivot

## 1. Description (Observation Only)

- A parallel price channel is shown with two boundary lines and a midpoint line between them.
- The speaker refers to the "midpoint of the parallel channel."
- The chart shows price interacting with the midpoint during multiple prior periods.
- The speaker states: "the midpoint is, if you look historically, it's been a pivot point."
- The chart shows price behavior at the midpoint that the speaker describes as pausing, resting, and then moving higher.
- The midpoint is referenced during a prior selloff and during a subsequent upward move.
- In response to "where am I buying with both hands?", the speaker references the midpoint of the parallel channel.

## 2. Interpretation (Speaker Intent)

- The speaker treats the midpoint of the channel as a meaningful internal level.
- The midpoint is used as a pivot area where price may pause, react, or reverse.
- In this example, the midpoint is also presented as a location where aggressive buying was justified by the speaker.

## 3. Inputs

- A parallel channel defined by two boundary lines.
- A midpoint line drawn between the channel boundaries.
- Historical price interactions with the midpoint.
- Current price location relative to the midpoint.
- Instrument and timeframe context shown on the chart.

## 4. Expected Behaviors

- Pause at the midpoint.
- Rest at the midpoint.
- Reaction at the midpoint.
- Movement higher after midpoint interaction.
- Pivot behavior at the midpoint across multiple periods.

## 7. Testable Hypotheses

- When price reaches the midpoint of a pre-defined parallel channel, the probability of a measurable reaction near that midpoint increases relative to baseline interactions at non-marked prices within the same channel.
- When price approaches a channel midpoint after a directional move, the probability of pausing near the midpoint increases relative to baseline movement through non-midpoint channel areas.
- When price interacts with a channel midpoint that has multiple prior historical contacts, the probability of a short-horizon directional change or pause increases relative to baseline midpoint interactions without such prior contacts.

## 8. Measurement Considerations

- The method for drawing the parallel channel must be defined before testing.
- The midpoint must be defined mechanically as the line equidistant from the two channel boundaries.
- "Reaction," "pause," and "rest" require explicit operational definitions and fixed lookahead windows.
- Tolerance around the midpoint must be defined as a band rather than assumed exact-line contact.
- Prior historical interaction count requires a source-consistent rule, which is not defined here.
- Testing should keep timeframe and instrument segmentation explicit.

## 9. Known Ambiguities / Gaps

- No explicit rule is given for how long the historical reference period should be.
- No threshold is given for what qualifies as a pivot interaction.

- No rule is given for distinguishing a midpoint reaction from normal within-channel movement.
